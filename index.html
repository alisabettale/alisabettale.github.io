Intro

I'm currently the Content & Instruction Coordinator for Berkeley Lab K-12 STEM Education & Outreach Programs. I develop curriculum and content that reflects innovative Berkeley Lab research and provides engaging and authentic STEM learning experiences for a K-12 audience.

Prior to joining Berkeley Lab, I taught high school physics for five years and also have experience teaching engineering and Earth Science. I have an EdM from Harvard Graduate School of Education, a MS in Astronomy from San Diego State University, and a BS in Physics (Astrophysics Track) from Carnegie Mellon University.

I was born and raised in Tokyo, Japan and grew up in a multicultural (Japanese / Polish / Russian) family. I love playing the drums, exploring local breweries with my husband Andrea, and playing with my feisty but sweet cat Stella.

Past

Teaching

When I was a graduate student at San Diego State University, I was asked to be a graduate TA for Astronomy 109 (Astronomy Laboratory). This was my first taste in lesson planning, differentiation, and grading! I ended up loving the experience so much that I decided to pursue a career in education.

I've taught all grades 9-12, usually in mixed-grade classes, including: AP Physics 1, AP Physics 2, Honors Physics, and Project Lead The Way (PLTW) Introduction to Engineering Design. In the middle school level I taught 8th grade Earth Science. I hold initial teaching licenses from Massachusetts for physics (grades 8-12) and technology/engineering (grades 5-12). Below are some of my sample lessons.

Engineering: Kuiper's Project


The Astronomy Club at Everett High School had an official mascot Kuiper the "Space Bunny" (a joke on the adorable looking sea bunny), and I had a plush of it in the classroom. One day, some students said that we should design some wheels so Kuiper could move on its own. What an awesome idea! I designed a group project where students would go through PLTW's engineering design process to design and create wheels for Kuiper. They designed the wheels through CAD (using Onshape), and then even got to 3D print them! During the project students learned about position-time graphs by making and analyzing one when testing their wheels.


Some 3D printed wheels. All are original designs of students!

Physics: Exoplanets


Image credit: NASA

I love to share my passion for astronomy with my students! I often connect what I'm teaching to real-life applications in astronomy. I adapted this lesson from NASA JPL for my Honors Physics class at Everett High School. This was a great application of Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation, as well as good practice calculating with scientific notation. The students loved working with real data and for most of them it was the first time they learned about the Kepler mission and exoplanets.
